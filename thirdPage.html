Temperature and Air Quality Analysis in each continent

Temperature vs. PM10 in cities where Air Quality Index is greater than or equal to 3

The analysis of the temperature and PM10 Air Quality Index (AQI) across cities in Asia, Europe, Africa, and the Americas reveals notable regional differences and trends.

Asia

In Asian cities, a positive correlation between temperature and PM10 AQI is evident, with higher temperatures often coinciding with elevated AQI levels. Cities like Beijing Shi and New Delhi stand out with significantly higher AQI values, reflecting the impacts of urbanization, industrial activity, and vehicular emissions. The trend in Asian cities suggests that warmer temperatures might exacerbate pollution levels, possibly due to increased photochemical reactions in urban areas.

Europe

In contrast to Asia, European cities such as Amsterdam, Rome, and Paris exhibit a narrower range of both temperature and AQI values. While there is still a positive relationship between the two variables, the lower AQI levels compared to Asian cities suggest better air quality management and stricter environmental regulations in Europe. The clustering of European cities within a mid-temperature range reflects a more uniform climate across the region, with fewer extreme pollution events.

Africa

African cities, including Cairo, Nairobi, and Pretoria, display a more dispersed distribution of temperature and AQI values. The broader range likely reflects the diverse climatic and environmental conditions across the continent. While some cities maintain relatively low AQI levels, others show higher values, potentially due to localized factors like urban density, industrial activity, and seasonal dust storms. Overall, the analysis highlights significant geographic variations in air quality trends, with Asian cities facing more severe pollution challenges, Europe maintaining stricter air quality standards, and Africa experiencing diverse air quality patterns driven by local conditions.

America

In the South and North American cities plot, a significant clustering of data points is observed at lower PM10 AQI values (below 200) across a temperature range of 15°C to 30°C, indicating relatively better air quality in many cities. However, a few cities, such as Lima, exhibit extremely high AQI values exceeding 1,000, predominantly in the cooler temperature range (6°C to 12°C), suggesting localized pollution challenges possibly influenced by atmospheric or topographical conditions. No clear correlation is evident between temperature and AQI, highlighting that air quality is driven more by region-specific factors like urban density, industrial activities, and policy measures. The analysis underscores significant variability across cities, with some regions facing severe air quality issues while others maintain relatively low AQI

It’s also important to note that in the provided analysis of air quality trends above, there are no Australian cities with a PM10 Air Quality Index (AQI) below 3, hence no plot could be rendered. This absence suggests that Australian cities consistently maintain lower PM10 AQI values. Such an omission highlights a potential area for further investigation, as Australia’s stringent environmental policies and its geographical isolation may contribute to cleaner air compared to regions with higher urban and industrial densities. This insight could offer valuable context when comparing global air quality trends and identifying best practices for pollution mitigation.

Air Quality - The Level of PM2.5 in Europe vs. Asia for different weather conditions

We have chosen to visualize data from Asia versus Europe as according to the Environmental Performance Index, which measures the direct impacts of air pollution on human health in each country, 4 out of the top 5 countries with the best weather performance score are in Europe, while the 5 countries with the lowest index are in Asia

The first bar plot demonstrates the level of PM2.5 in different weather conditions in capital cities of Asia and Europe. Overall, it is very clear that there is a distinguishable difference in the level of PM2.5- indicating a higher level of air pollution - across all weather types in Asia, compared to Europe. This pattern suggests that there is an uneven distribution of air pollution. The pattern observed above between Asia and Europe confirms what the Environmental Performance Index shows, as the levels of PM2.5 in capital cities in Asia are all higher than that in Europe. The detailed look into each continent's and even countries' pollution levels in their capital cities can help assess the effectiveness of their climate policies and push for stronger accountability in mitigating global climate effects.
