Global Weather Analysis: A Study of Capital Cities

Introduction

Weather plays a crucial role in our daily lives, influencing everything from agriculture and tourism to transportation and public health. Capital cities, as political, economic, and cultural hubs, often shape national policies in areas like urban planning, agriculture, and disaster preparedness. Their weather patterns can represent broader regions, especially in countries where significant populations live around the capital. This makes them valuable for studying national responses to climate change and regional impacts. This project aims to analyze a comprehensive dataset of daily weather information from capital cities around the world, starting from August 2023. With over 40 features—including temperature, wind, pressure, precipitation, humidity, visibility, and air quality measurements—this dataset enables the exploration of global weather trends and their relationships. By examining this data, we can uncover insights about climate variability, identify regions most affected by climate change, and provide valuable information for policymakers and the general public. Understanding these weather patterns can then help us predict climate trends, prepare for extreme weather events, and mitigate potential impacts on society.

To learn more about the relationship between climate variability and extreme weather events, you can visit Climate Change and Weather Extremes: A review of Recent Findings. Since climate data is collected across a large number of weather stations globally, it is important to understand the complexities involved in estimating global temperatures through collecting large-scale data, as well as potential methodologies for correcting biases and ensuring the accuracy of climate trend analysis through Estimating Sampling Errors in Large-Scale Temperature Averages

Introduction to the Data

Data source: Global Weather Repository

To investigate recent weather trends and patterns on a global scale, we have decided to examine the World Weather Repository dataset, which contains daily weather data for capital cities around the world, starting from August 2023. We have been able to obtain the dataset through Kaggle as a CSV file that contains a single table with 41 columns and 28,147 rows. For a specific day, there are 195 rows with weather condition measurements for capital cities of 195 countries around the world. This dataset provides 40+ features of the current weather and climate conditions globally, including crucial attributes that we are interested in such as temperature, weather condition, amount of precipitation, humidity, UV index, as well as air quality metrics for carbon monoxide, nitrogen oxide, PM 2.5, PM 10, sulfur dioxide, and the U.S. EPA air quality index. Most attributes of this dataset are quantitative (including attributes such as temperature, amount of precipitation, humidity, air quality measurements etc.), as they are measurements of different features of the weather in cities, however, there is still meaningful ordered (EPA air quality index, UV index, GB DEFRA Index) and unordered categorical data (weather condition), as well as date/time data that are relevant to our analysis.

Air Quality - The Level of PM2.5 in Europe vs. Asia for different weather conditions

The first bar plot demonstrates the level of PM2.5 in different weather conditions in capital cities of Asia and Europe. Overall, it is very clear that there is a distinguishable difference in the level of PM2.5- indicating a higher level of air pollution - across all weather types in Asia, compared to Europe. This pattern suggests that there is an uneven distribution of air pollution. We have chosen to visualize data from Asia versus Europe as according to the Environmental Performance Index, which measures the direct impacts of air pollution on human health in each country, 4 out of the top 5 countries with the best weather performance score are in Europe, while the 5 countries with the lowest index are in Asia, which further confirms the pattern shown in the bar plot, where the levels of PM2.5 in capital cities in Asia are all higher than that in Europe. pollution levels in their capital cities can help assess the effectiveness of their climate policies and push for stronger accountability in mitigating global climate effects.
